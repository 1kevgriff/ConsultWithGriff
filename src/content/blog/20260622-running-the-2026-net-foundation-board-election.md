Most folks have no idea how the .NET Foundation picks its Board of Directors. Honestly? A few years ago, neither did I.

This was my last election as President. I didn't run for re-election this cycle, and I wanted to leave the process better than I found it. So before I hand off the gavel, let me pull back the curtain on how the 2026 election actually came together. The good, the deliberate, and the one number I'm genuinely proud of.

## Why I cared about getting this one right

When you're stepping away, you get a choice. You can coast, or you can spend your last bit of political capital on something that outlasts you.

I picked the second one.

The board sets the direction for the entire Foundation, and the people on it matter more than any single decision they'll make. Three seats were open this cycle, with terms starting July 1st. Getting the right folks into those seats, and running a process that was actually *fair*, felt like the most useful thing I could do on my way out.

## Business over bits

Here's the thing: the .NET Foundation doesn't need seven more brilliant compiler engineers on its board.

It needs people who know how to grow a community, land sponsorships, market what we do, and run a nonprofit without setting the money on fire. So this cycle, we said it out loud. We prioritized **business expertise over technical depth**. Sales, marketing, community growth, partnership development, nonprofit governance.

That's not a knock on technical folks. I'm one of them. But a board is a leadership body, not a code review. The skills that make someone a great architect aren't the skills that keep a foundation solvent and visible.

## We didn't wait for nominations. We recruited.

If you open a form and wait, you get whoever happens to be paying attention that week. Usually that's the same handful of people who always run.

So we went out and recruited. I personally reached out to people I thought would be a great fit and talked a bunch of them into throwing their hat in. Some said yes. Some said no. A few went from "I don't think so, Kevin" to self-nominating a week later.

That outreach is the single biggest lever we have, and I don't think we use it enough.

## STV, and why we required a full ballot this time

Let's talk about counting votes, because this part matters more than people think.

We've always counted with **Scottish STV**, the single transferable vote. You rank candidates in order of preference, and opavote (the service we use) does the math. To win a seat, a candidate has to clear a vote threshold called the quota, which for three seats works out to just over 25%. That part isn't new, and it's a big reason these elections are fair.

In a plain "vote for your top three" election, a crowded ballot is dangerous. Too many similar candidates split the vote, and somebody can win, or lose, purely because of who *else* was on the ballot. That's the spoiler effect, and it punishes a healthy, competitive field.

> With STV, there's **no spoiler effect**. You rank candidates in order of preference, and if your top choice is eliminated or has more than enough votes to win, your vote transfers to your next choice. Nobody is "wasted."

Here's what *was* new this cycle: for the first time, we **required a full ballot**. You had to rank every candidate, not just your top few.

Why bother? Because partial ballots get *exhausted*. If you only rank two people and both are out of the running, your ballot stops transferring. It goes quiet for the rest of the count. Require a full ranking and every ballot stays live through every round. The final seats get decided by real majorities instead of thinning piles of votes.

It's a little more work at the keyboard. But it means your full set of preferences actually counts, all the way down. Worth it.

## The ballot-viability gate

We didn't want a ballot of thirty names, though. Nobody ranks thirty people honestly.

We started with an official intake of **18 nominees**. From there, four ballot-viability questions gated who actually appeared on the ballot. Basic confirmations that someone understood the commitment and could realistically serve. Incumbents seeking re-election were automatically on and exempt from the gate.

My working target was a field of about **nine candidates**, roughly three times the number of open seats. That's the sweet spot I landed on: competitive enough to give voters a real choice, small enough that ranking everyone is actually doable.

| | This cycle |
|---|---|
| Open seats | 3 |
| Term length | 3 years (starts July 1, 2026) |
| Nominees at intake | 18 |
| Target ballot size | ~9 (≈3× seats) |
| Eligible voters | ~1,629 |
| Counting method | Scottish STV (ranked choice) |

> One eligibility note that surprises people: Microsoft full-time employees can't run for an elected seat. Microsoft already holds an appointed seat on the board, so this keeps the elected side independent.

## A stronger field, top to bottom

All that recruiting and gating did what it was supposed to. We ended up with a field that had real range: business operators, community organizers, folks who live in partnerships and sponsorships. People who'd actually move the Foundation forward.

The composition got better too. The new board seats three women across seven elected spots, up from the two we'd been parked at for a few cycles. Good to see. But I don't want to oversell one stat, because the real win is simpler: voters had a genuinely strong, varied slate to rank, and the people who came out on top earned every vote.

That's the whole game. Build a better field, and good outcomes tend to follow.

## Your 2026 winners

Enough process talk. Here are the people who actually won:

- **[Meagon Hansen](https://www.linkedin.com/in/81megs/)**: a new director bringing exactly the sales and marketing background this cycle was built around.
- **[Hayden Barnes](https://www.linkedin.com/in/thbarnes/)**: a new director whose partnerships and open-source-sustainability work lines up squarely with where the Foundation needs to head.
- **[Mitchel Sellers](https://www.linkedin.com/in/mitchelsellers/)**: re-elected incumbent and current VP. Three more years of governance continuity, and the membership clearly wanted it.

Welcome aboard, all three. You're going to do great.

## Stepping away

Running an organization's election while you're on your way out the door is a strange feeling. You're building something you won't get to use.

But that's kind of the whole point of community work, isn't it? You leave the campsite better than you found it and trust the next folks to keep the fire going. I've tried to do that here: a fairer count, a more intentional ballot, and a stronger board to carry the Foundation forward.

I've spent a lot of my career on community work, from running the [Hampton Roads .NET User Group since 2009 and organizing DevFest](/hampton-roads-devfest-2024-retrospective) to [accidentally falling into leadership roles I never planned for](/azure-cloud-talk-accidental-cto). This one's near the top of the list.
